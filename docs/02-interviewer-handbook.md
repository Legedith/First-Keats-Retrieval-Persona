# Interviewer Handbook

## The interviewer’s job

The interviewer’s job is to create conditions where the narrator can safely remember, choose, explain, refuse, and revise.

The interviewer is:

- A listener.
- A facilitator.
- A witness.
- A temporary custodian of sensitive material.

The interviewer is not:

- A therapist.
- A judge.
- A prosecutor.
- A journalist chasing revelation.
- A family delegate trying to settle disputes.
- A ghostwriter trying to produce a neat story.

## Before the interview

Do not start recording until the narrator understands the purpose.

Cover:

1. Why you want to record.
2. Who may hear it.
3. Whether the narrator can restrict parts.
4. What topics are off-limits.
5. How to pause or stop.
6. Whether the narrator can review recordings or notes.
7. Whether the narrator wants a family member, neutral interviewer, or private self-recording for some topics.
8. Whether any materials should be sealed until a date or condition.

Use [`templates/pre_interview_agreement.md`](../templates/pre_interview_agreement.md).

## Session length

Prefer several short sessions over one long session.

Suggested length:

- First session: 30–60 minutes.
- Later sessions: 45–90 minutes.
- Sensitive sessions: shorter, with more control points.

Stop before exhaustion. A tired narrator may agree to things they would otherwise refuse.

## Opening the conversation

Begin gently.

Possible opening:

> “I am not here to test your memory. I am here to understand how you remember your life. You can skip anything, correct me, pause, or stop.”

Then ask:

> “If your life were divided into chapters, what would the chapters be called?”

or:

> “Please tell me the story of your life in whatever way makes sense to you. You can begin wherever you like.”

## The grammar of good questions

### Ask one question at a time

Bad:

> “Where were you living then, who was with you, and was that when you met your wife?”

Better:

> “Where were you living then?”

Then wait.

### Prefer open invitations

Use:

- “Tell me about…”
- “Describe…”
- “What do you remember about…”
- “How did that unfold?”
- “What was that like?”
- “Can you give me an example?”

Avoid questions that can be answered only with yes or no unless you need a simple clarification.

### Use the narrator’s own words

Narrator:

> “That was when things became heavy.”

Good follow-up:

> “What do you mean by ‘heavy’?”

Bad follow-up:

> “So you became depressed?”

### Do not smuggle in assumptions

Bad:

> “Why did your father refuse to help?”

This assumes refusal and motive.

Better:

> “What role did your father have in that situation?”

### Avoid emotional labeling

Bad:

> “Were you devastated?”

Better:

> “What was happening inside you then, as far as you remember?”

### Ask for scenes

Narrator:

> “Our teacher was strict.”

Good follow-up:

> “Can you remember one particular moment that shows what strict meant?”

### Let silence work

After a meaningful question, count slowly in your head before speaking again. Many memories need silence.

Do not rescue every pause.

## The story-deepening ladder

Use this ladder when a memory seems important.

You do not need every rung.

### 1. Locate

> “Roughly when was this?”  
> “Where were you living then?”

### 2. Particularize

> “Is there one particular day or incident that comes to mind?”

### 3. Unfold

> “What happened first?”  
> “What happened after that?”

### 4. Populate

> “Who was there?”  
> “Who mattered most in that moment?”

### 5. Contextualize

> “What would have been considered normal then?”  
> “What choices did someone in your position actually have?”

### 6. Experience

> “What were you noticing?”  
> “What did you think was going to happen?”

### 7. Interpret

> “What did it mean to you then?”  
> “Do you see it differently now?”

### 8. Qualify

> “Is this something you remember directly, something you were told, or a mixture?”

## Human branching rules

Use these rules while listening.

If the answer is broad, ask for one example.

If the example is interesting, ask for the sequence.

If the sequence is clear, ask about people.

If the people are clear, ask about relationships and power.

If the relationship is clear, ask how it changed.

If the event is clear, ask what it meant then.

If the meaning is clear, ask whether it means something different now.

If the narrator hesitates, offer a choice.

If the narrator refuses, move on.

If the narrator becomes tired, stop.

If you notice a contradiction, write it down and revisit later without accusation.

If you feel the urge to explain the narrator’s life back to them, slow down.

## Following tangents

A tangent is not automatically a problem.

Follow the tangent if it has:

- Emotion.
- Detail.
- Repeated names.
- Humor.
- Surprise.
- A phrase the narrator emphasizes.
- A connection to an underexplored period.
- A person who has not yet appeared in the life map.

Return to the outline later:

> “I want to return to what you said about your first job, but before that, this story about your neighbor seems important. Can we stay with it for a moment?”

## Handling difficult moments

When the narrator becomes upset:

> “Take your time. Would you like to continue, pause, change the subject, or stop for today?”

Do not say:

> “It is good to let it out.”

You do not know that.

Do not say:

> “This will help you heal.”

This is not therapy.

Do not say:

> “But people need to know.”

Agency matters more than completeness.

## Ending a session

Do not end immediately after a painful disclosure.

Move to neutral ground:

> “Before we stop, is there a lighter memory from that period that you would like preserved too?”

or:

> “What would feel like a good place to pause today?”

Then ask:

> “Is there anything from today that you want kept private, corrected, or not used?”

## After the interview

Immediately write:

- Date.
- Location.
- Who was present.
- Mood and energy.
- Topics covered.
- Topics avoided.
- Restrictions.
- Open threads.
- Names and places needing confirmation.
- Follow-up questions.
- Anything the narrator asked to change, remove, or seal.

Use [`templates/session_notes.md`](../templates/session_notes.md).

## Common interviewer mistakes

### Mistake 1: Chasing drama

The most dramatic story is not always the most representative story.

### Mistake 2: Overpraising disclosure

Saying “thank you for being so brave” after every hard story can pressure the narrator to keep revealing more.

### Mistake 3: Treating brevity as failure

A short answer may mean the topic is private, unimportant, painful, forgotten, or simply finished.

### Mistake 4: Asking adult children’s questions

Children often ask parents questions that are really about themselves.

Example:

> “Did you love me less when I was young?”

That may be valid in a private family conversation, but it changes the purpose of the life interview.

### Mistake 5: Fact-checking too early

Do not interrupt emotional recall with:

> “Was that 1976 or 1978?”

Mark it for later.

## The interviewer’s self-check

Before asking the next question, ask yourself:

1. Am I following the narrator or dragging them?
2. Am I asking one thing?
3. Did I introduce a fact they did not say?
4. Did I introduce an emotion they did not name?
5. Is this question for their story or for my curiosity?
6. Is the narrator still willing and alert?
7. Have I left space for silence?
