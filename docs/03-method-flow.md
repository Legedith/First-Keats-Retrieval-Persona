# Method Flow

The Life Narrative Elicitation Method is a spiral, not a questionnaire.

You begin broadly, deepen selected material, return later with a map, fill important gaps, and give the narrator the chance to review, restrict, correct, and reinterpret.

## Overview

```text
0. Agreement and boundaries
1. Free life narrative
2. Life map
3. Chapter interviews
4. Thematic passes
5. Artifact-led sessions
6. Review and narrator authority
```

## Phase 0: Agreement and boundaries

Purpose: establish consent, control, and scope.

Ask:

> “What would make these conversations worthwhile for you?”  
> “What do you most want preserved?”  
> “Are there topics I should not raise?”  
> “Are there topics where I should ask permission before going further?”  
> “Who may hear these recordings?”  
> “Can you change your mind later?”

Output:

- Consent notes.
- Topic boundaries.
- Sharing rules.
- Session length.
- Review preference.

Do not treat this as paperwork only. This is part of the method.

## Phase 1: Free life narrative

Purpose: let the narrator define the story before the interviewer imposes structure.

Opening prompt:

> “Please tell me the story of your life in whatever way makes sense to you. You can begin wherever you like. I will mostly listen, and afterward I may ask about things you mentioned.”

Alternative:

> “If your life were a book, what would its chapters be called?”

During this phase:

- Interrupt rarely.
- Note phrases, people, places, dates, and emotional centers.
- Do not correct sequence.
- Do not fill gaps.
- Do not compare with what others have said.

Output:

- First narrator-defined structure.
- Initial chapter names.
- Important people and places.
- Topics with energy.
- Avoided or closed topics.
- Open threads.

## Phase 2: Life map

Purpose: create a rough structure across time and domains.

The life map is not a finished timeline. It is a working aid.

Domains:

- Places lived.
- Households.
- Education.
- Work.
- Caregiving.
- Marriage, partnership, family.
- Friendship and community.
- Health.
- Faith and belief.
- Public and historical events.
- Money and material life.
- Major losses, changes, migrations, and transitions.

Ask:

> “During that period, where were you living?”  
> “Who was in the household?”  
> “What were you doing most days?”  
> “Who had the biggest influence on you then?”  
> “What else was happening in the world around you?”

Output:

- Approximate periods.
- Known gaps.
- Uncertain dates.
- Domains needing follow-up.

Use [`templates/life_map.md`](../templates/life_map.md).

## Phase 3: Chapter interviews

Purpose: explore each major period with depth.

For each chapter, ask through seven lenses:

1. World.
2. People.
3. Doing.
4. Choosing.
5. Experiencing.
6. Changing.
7. Looking back.

Example chapter opener:

> “Let us open the chapter you called ‘the factory years.’ When you say that phrase, what is the first place or scene that comes to mind?”

For each chapter, try to capture:

- One ordinary day.
- One specific scene.
- One important relationship.
- One constraint.
- One choice.
- One change.
- One present-day interpretation.

Do not force all seven if the narrator resists.

## Phase 4: Thematic passes

Purpose: catch the things chronology misses.

Suggested thematic sessions:

1. Ordinary life and domestic routines.
2. Work, money, and material conditions.
3. Relationships and estrangements.
4. Love, marriage, intimacy, and family formation, if permitted.
5. Migration, place, language, and belonging.
6. Religion, ethics, values, and belief.
7. Public history as personally experienced.
8. Health, body, caregiving, disability, and aging.
9. Humor, play, mischief, and friendship.
10. Loss, regret, injustice, and unresolved conflict.
11. Skills, recipes, songs, tools, crafts, and disappearing knowledge.
12. Legacy and messages to future listeners.

Thematic passes prevent the method from becoming a sequence of milestones.

## Phase 5: Artifact-led sessions

Purpose: use objects as memory cues while recording their influence.

Artifacts may include:

- Photographs.
- Letters.
- Documents.
- Recipes.
- Songs.
- Clothes.
- Tools.
- Maps.
- Objects from a home or workplace.
- Places visited in person.

Start open:

> “What comes to mind when you look at this?”

Only later ask specifics.

Record the artifact cue:

> “This memory was told while looking at photo P12.”

Do not use artifacts to force agreement.

Bad:

> “This photo proves you were happy then, right?”

Better:

> “What do you notice in this photo now?”

## Phase 6: Review and narrator authority

Purpose: give the narrator the last word over representation and access.

Ask:

> “What did I misunderstand?”  
> “What should I ask again?”  
> “Which part should remain private?”  
> “Which part should be corrected?”  
> “Which uncertainty should stay uncertain?”  
> “What is missing?”  
> “What would you want someone to understand about you after listening?”

Output:

- Corrections.
- Restrictions.
- Follow-up needs.
- Approved themes.
- Material to seal or remove.
- Final narrator messages.

## Suggested session sequence

### Session 1: Orientation and life chapters

- Consent and boundaries.
- Life-as-book prompt.
- Broad first narrative.
- Light follow-ups.
- End with review.

### Session 2: Childhood and household world

- Home.
- Family members.
- Ordinary day.
- School or early work.
- Local rules and customs.

### Session 3: Adolescence and early adulthood

- Friends.
- Education or work.
- First major choices.
- Rules around gender, respectability, money, and mobility.
- One turning point.

### Session 4: Work, marriage, migration, or adult responsibilities

Pick whichever became central in the life map.

### Session 5: Relationships

- Parents.
- Siblings.
- Spouse or partner.
- Children.
- Friends.
- Mentors.
- Neighbors.
- Estrangements.

### Session 6: Ordinary life and culture

- Food.
- Festivals.
- Prayer.
- Songs.
- Repairs.
- Transport.
- News.
- Jokes.
- Community.

### Session 7: Harder material, only if permitted

- Loss.
- Regret.
- injustice.
- illness.
- conflict.
- shame.
- unfinished business.

The narrator can refuse the whole session.

### Session 8: Legacy and review

- Corrections.
- Restrictions.
- Messages.
- What matters most.
- What should not be simplified.

## When to stop

Stop when:

- The narrator is tired.
- The narrator is repeating because of fatigue.
- The narrator asks to stop.
- The interviewer feels emotionally overloaded.
- The session has reached the agreed time.
- The conversation has entered sensitive material without enough consent.
- The narrator says, directly or indirectly, “not now.”

A good stopping point protects the next conversation.
