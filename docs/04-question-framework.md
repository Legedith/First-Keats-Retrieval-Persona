# Question Framework

This is not a fixed questionnaire.

It is a way to choose the next question.

## The core question

At every moment, the interviewer asks:

> “What kind of question would help the narrator tell this life more truthfully, specifically, and safely?”

Not:

> “What question do I want answered next?”

## The seven question modes

### 1. Map mode

Use when you need broad structure.

Purpose: identify periods, people, places, and transitions.

Examples:

> “What would you call that period of your life?”  
> “Where were you living then?”  
> “Who was part of your daily life?”  
> “What changed at the end of that period?”

### 2. Scene mode

Use when a general statement needs concrete life.

Purpose: move from summary to event.

Examples:

> “Can you remember one particular day that shows what that was like?”  
> “Where are you in that memory?”  
> “What happened first?”  
> “Who else was there?”

### 3. Ordinary-life mode

Use when the interview is becoming too milestone-driven.

Purpose: preserve daily texture.

Examples:

> “What did a normal morning look like?”  
> “How did people get water, food, news, or transport?”  
> “What sounds belonged to that place?”  
> “What did people do for fun?”

### 4. Relationship mode

Use when a person matters but the relationship is flat.

Purpose: show change, power, care, conflict, and dependence.

Examples:

> “When did you first understand that person differently?”  
> “What did they teach you without saying it directly?”  
> “Was there a period when you were not close?”  
> “What remained unsaid between you?”

### 5. Context mode

Use when the story is too individualistic.

Purpose: restore social, historical, and material conditions.

Examples:

> “What choices were realistically available to someone like you then?”  
> “What would have been considered respectable or shameful?”  
> “Who had authority in that situation?”  
> “What was happening in the wider world?”

### 6. Meaning mode

Use after a story has been told clearly enough.

Purpose: capture interpretation.

Examples:

> “What did this mean to you at the time?”  
> “Has the meaning changed?”  
> “What do you think your younger self did not understand?”  
> “Why does this story stay with you?”

### 7. Review mode

Use near the end.

Purpose: preserve agency, correction, and access decisions.

Examples:

> “What did I misunderstand?”  
> “What should stay private?”  
> “What should I ask about next time?”  
> “What should remain uncertain rather than guessed?”

## Choosing the next question

Use this decision path.

### If the answer is too broad

Ask for an example.

Narrator:

> “Life was hard then.”

Question:

> “Can you remember one particular incident that shows what hard meant?”

### If the answer is a specific incident

Ask for sequence.

> “What happened first?”

### If the sequence is clear

Ask who was involved.

> “Who else was there?”

### If the people are clear

Ask about relationship.

> “What was your relationship with them like at that time?”

### If the personal view is clear

Ask about social context.

> “What would people around you have expected you to do?”

### If context is clear

Ask about meaning.

> “How do you understand that choice now?”

### If emotion rises

Ask for control, not more detail.

> “Would you like to stay with this, pause, change topics, or stop?”

### If the narrator says “I do not know”

Respect it.

Optional:

> “Would you like to leave it there, or would it help to talk around the period more generally?”

### If the narrator gives a short answer

One gentle follow-up is enough.

> “Is there anything more you want to say about that?”

If not, move on.

## The forbidden moves

Do not ask questions that:

- Insert a fact.
- Insert an emotion.
- Insert a motive.
- Insert another person’s version.
- Demand justification.
- Pressure disclosure.
- Turn pain into a lesson.
- Treat contradiction as lying.
- Ask multiple things at once.
- Use “surely,” “obviously,” “must have,” or “you must have felt.”
- Force exactness where the narrator is uncertain.

## Good question shapes

### “Tell me about…”

Best for opening a topic.

> “Tell me about the first house you remember.”

### “Can you take me to…”

Best for scene detail.

> “Can you take me to the day you left home?”

### “What was considered…”

Best for social context.

> “What was considered a good marriage then?”

### “Who…”

Best for relationship maps.

> “Who did you rely on most?”

### “When did you first…”

Best for change.

> “When did you first feel like an adult?”

### “What changed…”

Best for transitions.

> “What changed after your father died?”

### “How do you see it now…”

Best for meaning.

> “How do you see that decision now?”

### “Should we…”

Best for agency.

> “Should we keep going, or return to this another day?”

## Unsafe question rewrites

| Unsafe | Safer |
|---|---|
| “Were you lonely after marriage?” | “What was daily life like after marriage?” |
| “Why did your mother treat you badly?” | “How would you describe your relationship with your mother then?” |
| “Did poverty make you ashamed?” | “What did money mean in the family at that time?” |
| “Were you traumatized by the accident?” | “What do you remember about the period after the accident?” |
| “Your brother said you were angry. Is that true?” | “What do you remember feeling or thinking at that time?” |
| “Did you regret having children so early?” | “How did becoming a parent change your life then?” |
| “Was caste the reason they rejected you?” | “What factors do you think shaped their reaction?” |
| “Did you love your wife?” | “How did your relationship with her change over time?” |

## The depth rule

Do not go deeper just because you can.

Go deeper when:

- The narrator is willing.
- The topic seems meaningful to them.
- You have enough context.
- The question will not introduce assumptions.
- The session has enough time to close safely.

Stay lighter when:

- The narrator is tired.
- The topic was not invited.
- The answer was brief twice.
- The story involves living people and privacy risks.
- The interviewer is emotionally entangled.
