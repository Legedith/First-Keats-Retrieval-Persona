# Question Bank

Use this as a library, not a script.

Pick questions based on the narrator’s answers, boundaries, energy, relationship with the interviewer, and cultural context. Rephrase in the narrator’s language. Do not ask every question. Do not treat this file as a form to complete.

A useful life interview usually needs fewer questions asked better.

## Safety rule before any module

Before asking a question, check:

1. Am I adding a fact they did not say?
2. Am I adding an emotion they did not name?
3. Am I adding a motive they did not claim?
4. Am I using another person’s account before collecting this person’s own account?
5. Am I asking two or more questions at once?
6. Am I asking because the narrator’s story needs it, or because I am curious?
7. Has the narrator given permission for this depth?

If the answer is risky, rewrite the question.

## Universal follow-up ladder

Use this ladder with almost any answer.

### If the narrator gives a broad summary

> “Can you remember one particular moment that shows what that was like?”  
> “Was there a day, place, or person that captures that period?”  
> “Can you give me an example?”

### If the narrator names a period

> “What would you call that chapter of your life?”  
> “Where were you living then?”  
> “Who was part of daily life?”  
> “What did most days revolve around?”  
> “What changed at the end of that period?”

### If the narrator names a person

> “What was your relationship with them like at that time?”  
> “How did they usually speak to you?”  
> “What did you learn from them?”  
> “Did that relationship change later?”  
> “What remains unsaid about them?”

### If the narrator names a place

> “When you picture that place, where do you find yourself standing?”  
> “What did that place sound like on an ordinary day?”  
> “Who belonged there?”  
> “Who felt like an outsider there?”  
> “What has changed about that place?”

### If the narrator names an event

> “Can you take me through what happened, step by step?”  
> “What happened before that?”  
> “Who was present?”  
> “What did you understand at the time?”  
> “What changed after that?”

### If the narrator gives an emotion

> “What made it feel that way?”  
> “Where were you when you remember feeling that?”  
> “Did you show that feeling, or keep it private?”  
> “Did you understand that feeling the same way then as you do now?”

### If the narrator gives uncertainty

> “Should we mark that as approximate?”  
> “Is this direct memory, something you were told, or a mixture?”  
> “Would it help to leave the date open for now?”  
> “Who else might remember this differently?”

### If the narrator hesitates

> “We can leave this here, return later, or move to another topic. What would you prefer?”

## Opening the whole project

### Purpose and control

> “What would make these conversations worthwhile for you?”  
> “What do you most want preserved?”  
> “What do you not want this project to become?”  
> “Who do you imagine listening to this someday?”  
> “What should they understand about you?”  
> “What should they not assume about you?”  
> “Are there topics you want to avoid completely?”  
> “Are there topics where I should ask permission before continuing?”  
> “How will you tell me if you want to pause?”  
> “Would you prefer some topics to be discussed with someone else or privately recorded?”

### First narrative invitation

> “Please tell me the story of your life in whatever way makes sense to you.”  
> “You can begin wherever you like.”  
> “If your life were divided into chapters, what would those chapters be called?”  
> “What chapter should we open first?”  
> “Is there a story about you that people usually get wrong?”  
> “Is there a story about you that people usually do not ask about?”  
> “What should I be careful not to simplify?”

### Gentle first-session prompts

> “What is one place that made you who you are?”  
> “Who is one person I cannot understand your life without knowing about?”  
> “What is one ordinary memory that still feels alive?”  
> “What is one object, song, food, or smell that carries your past?”  
> “What is one chapter you are comfortable discussing today?”

### First-session closure

> “How was this conversation for you?”  
> “Was anything too much?”  
> “What should I ask about next time?”  
> “Is anything from today private?”  
> “Did I misunderstand anything?”

> “Can you take me to one day from this chapter?”

> “What was an unusual day like?”

> “What did people expect from you?”

> “What did your younger self misunderstand?”

> “If this chapter had a title in your own language, what would it be?”

## Childhood and home

### First memories

> “What is the first place you remember?”  
> “What is the first person you remember clearly?”  
> “What is the first sound, smell, or image that comes to mind from childhood?”  
> “What is a memory you are not sure is yours, but has always been told in the family?”  
> “Who usually tells childhood stories about you?”

### Home world

> “When you picture your childhood home, where are you standing?”  
> “What did the entrance look like?”  
> “Where did people sit?”  
> “Where did people sleep?”  
> “Where was food prepared?”  
> “Where did children play?”  
> “Where did adults talk privately?”  
> “What part of the house felt safe?”  
> “What part of the house felt forbidden?”  
> “What did the house sound like in the morning?”  
> “What did it sound like at night?”

### Childhood ordinary life

> “What did a normal morning look like?”  
> “Who woke up first?”  
> “Who cooked?”  
> “Who cleaned?”  
> “Who fetched water, fuel, food, or supplies?”  
> “What chores did children do?”  
> “What did children do after school or work?”  
> “What games did children play?”  
> “What did you do when adults were not watching?”  
> “What did you wear on ordinary days?”  
> “What did you wear for special occasions?”  
> “What did you eat when times were good?”  
> “What did you eat when times were difficult?”

### Rules and authority

> “Who had authority in the house?”  
> “Who made decisions?”  
> “Who was feared?”  
> “Who was trusted?”  
> “What were children not allowed to say?”  
> “What were children not allowed to know?”  
> “How were children corrected?”  
> “What counted as good behavior?”  
> “What counted as shameful behavior?”  
> “Did rules differ for boys and girls?”  
> “Did rules differ by age or birth order?”

### Inner childhood

> “What kind of child were you?”  
> “Were you seen that way by others?”  
> “What did adults praise you for?”  
> “What did adults criticize you for?”  
> “What did you want that you could not ask for?”

> “What made you proud?”  
> “What made you feel small?”  
> “Who understood you best?”  
> “Who misunderstood you?”

## Parents, elders, and ancestors

> “What did she teach directly?”

> “When did you first understand her as a person, not only as a mother?”  
> “What did she carry silently?”  
> “What did she want for you?”  
> “What did you inherit from her?”  
> “What did you decide not to repeat?”

### Father or paternal figures

> “How would you describe your father or paternal figure when you were young?”  
> “What was his daily life like?”  
> “What did people expect from him?”  
> “What did he teach directly?”  
> “What did he teach without saying it?”  
> “What was easy with him?”  
> “What was difficult?”  
> “When did you first see his vulnerability or limits?”  
> “What did he worry about, as far as you understood?”  
> “What did you inherit from him?”  
> “What did you reject?”

### Grandparents and elders

> “Which elder shaped you most?”  
> “What did they know that others did not?”  
> “What stories did they repeat?”

> “What did they never talk about?”  
> “How did they show love?”  
> “How did they show authority?”  
> “What did they remember from before your time?”

> “What do you wish you had asked them?”

## Work and livelihood

> “What was your first work, paid or unpaid?”

> “Who taught you what to do?”

### Workday detail

> “What time did the day begin?”  
> “How did you get to work?”

> “What did your hands do most often?”  
> “What did your body have to endure?”  
> “Who gave instructions?”  
> “Who watched the work?”  
> “Where did people rest?”

> “Was that change chosen or forced?”

## Migration, place, and language

### Leaving and arriving

> “Which places made you who you are?”  
> “Can you tell me about the day you left?”  
> “What did you carry?”  
> “Who came with you?”  
> “Who stayed behind?”  
> “What did you expect before arriving?”  
> “What surprised you after arriving?”

> “When did you feel like you belonged?”  
> “When did you feel like an outsider?”  
> “What gave you away as new or different?”  
> “Did accent, clothing, food, name, caste, class, religion, or language matter?”  
> “What did you change to fit in?”

> “What sounds belonged there?”  
> “What route did you walk often?”  
> “What place no longer exists?”

## Difficult chapters and unresolved material

Use only with permission. The goal is not to extract pain. The goal is to give the narrator control over whether and how difficult material is preserved.

### Permission

> “Would it be okay to ask about that period, or should we leave it?”  
> “Would you prefer to speak generally rather than in detail?”  
> “Would you prefer to pause before continuing?”  
> “Should this topic be kept private even if we discuss it?”

### Gentle entry

> “How do you refer to that period in your own mind?”  
> “What changed in daily life?”  
> “Who helped?”  
> “Who was absent?”  
> “What did people understand at the time?”  
> “What did people misunderstand?”  
> “What was not safe or possible to say?”

### Regret and repair

> “Is there a decision you still think about?”  
> “Is there something you wish people understood more kindly?”  
> “Is there something you have made peace with?”  
> “Is there something you have not made peace with?”  
> “Was repair possible?”  
> “Was repair attempted?”  
> “Is there anything that should not be turned into a lesson?”

### Closing difficult material

> “Would you like this kept, restricted, or removed?”  
> “Would you like to move to a lighter memory before we stop?”  
> “What would feel like a safe place to pause?”

## Counter-script questions

These questions resist the standard biography pattern.

### What normal biographies miss

> “What part of your life would a normal biography leave out?”  
> “What did you do every day that nobody thought worth recording?”  
> “Who mattered greatly but would not appear on a family tree?”  
> “What did women, workers, children, elders, outsiders, or quiet people understand that others missed?”  
> “What did you know that educated or powerful people did not know?”

### Hidden lives and social limits

> “What could someone like you not openly want?”  
> “What dream would have seemed unrealistic or improper?”  
> “What part of yourself did you keep private?”  
> “What did your world make difficult to say?”  
> “When did you have to act differently from what you felt?”

### Against simplification

> “What should we resist simplifying about your life?”  
> “What is both true and hard to explain?”  
> “What story has more than one version?”  
> “What do people praise but misunderstand?”  
> “What do people criticize but misunderstand?”

### What should remain

## Review and close

Use these at the end of every session.

### Accuracy

> “What did I misunderstand?”  
> “Are there names, places, dates, or relationships to correct?”  
> “Is anything approximate?”  
> “Is anything based on family story rather than direct memory?”  
> “Is there another version of this story?”

### Access and privacy

> “Is there anything from today that should remain private?”  
> “Is there anything that should be sealed until later?”  
> “Is there anything that should not be used?”  
> “Who may hear this?”  
> “Who should not hear this?”

### Emotional closure

> “How was this conversation for you?”  
> “Was anything too much?”  
> “Would you like the next conversation to be lighter, deeper, or different?”  
> “Would you like to end with a lighter memory?”  
> “What feels like a good place to stop?”

### Next session

> “What should I ask next time?”  
> “What should I not ask next time?”  
> “Is there an object, photograph, song, place, or person that would help next time?”  
> “Would another interviewer be better for any topic?”
