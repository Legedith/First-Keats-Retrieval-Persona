# Question Bank

Use this as a library, not a script.

Pick questions based on the narrator’s answers, boundaries, and energy. Rephrase in the narrator’s language.

## Opening questions

> “What would make these conversations worthwhile for you?”  
> “What do you most want preserved?”  
> “If your life were divided into chapters, what would those chapters be called?”  
> “Where would you like to begin?”  
> “Is there a period of your life people usually ask about too much or too little?”  
> “What should I be careful not to simplify about you?”

## Life chapters

> “What would you call this chapter?”  
> “When did this chapter begin for you?”  
> “What changed at the end of it?”  
> “Who were the main people in that chapter?”  
> “Where were you living?”  
> “What did most days revolve around?”  
> “What did you want then?”  
> “What were you responsible for?”  
> “What did you not yet understand?”  
> “What do you understand differently now?”

## Childhood

> “What is the first home you remember?”  
> “When you picture that home, where are you standing?”  
> “Who was usually there?”  
> “What did mornings look like?”  
> “What sounds belonged to that place?”  
> “What did people eat on ordinary days?”  
> “Who had authority in the house?”  
> “What were children allowed to do?”  
> “What were children not allowed to say?”  
> “Who comforted you?”  
> “Who scared you?”  
> “What games did children play?”  
> “What did you know about money then?”  
> “What did you think adults were hiding from children?”  
> “What did you misunderstand about the world?”

## Parents and elders

> “How would you describe your mother or maternal figure when you were young?”  
> “How would you describe your father or paternal figure when you were young?”  
> “When did you first see them as people, not only as parents?”  
> “What did they teach directly?”  
> “What did they teach by example?”  
> “What did you inherit from them?”  
> “What did you reject?”  
> “What did you understand about them only after becoming older?”  
> “Was there something they never spoke about?”  
> “What do you wish you had asked them?”

## Siblings and cousins

> “Who were you closest to as a child?”  
> “Who protected whom?”  
> “Who fought most?”  
> “Were there roles among the siblings?”  
> “Did birth order matter?”  
> “Did boys and girls have different freedoms?”  
> “What changed as you grew older?”  
> “Was there a sibling or cousin who understood you best?”  
> “Was there a relationship that became distant?”

## School and learning

> “What do you remember about your first school?”  
> “What did the classroom look like?”  
> “Who was a teacher you still remember?”  
> “What were you good at?”  
> “What were you afraid of?”  
> “How were children disciplined?”  
> “What did education mean in your family?”  
> “Who expected you to study?”  
> “Who expected you not to?”  
> “What did you learn outside school?”  
> “What skill are you proud of learning?”

## Work and livelihood

> “What was your first paid work?”  
> “How did you get it?”  
> “What did a normal workday look like?”  
> “Who trained you?”  
> “What did you have to learn quickly?”  
> “What was considered good work?”  
> “What was unfair?”  
> “Who had power over your life at work?”  
> “How did money move through the household?”  
> “What did you buy when you first had your own money?”  
> “What work did you do that nobody called work?”  
> “What changed in you because of work?”

## Money and material life

> “What did money mean in your family?”  
> “Who controlled money?”  
> “What was expensive then?”  
> “What was saved, repaired, reused, or borrowed?”  
> “Was debt discussed openly?”  
> “What did people do in a crisis?”  
> “What did respectability require?”  
> “What object did your family value most?”  
> “When did life become materially easier or harder?”

## Migration and place

> “Which places made you who you are?”  
> “Can you tell me about the day you left?”  
> “What did you carry?”  
> “Who came with you?”  
> “Who was left behind?”  
> “When did the new place begin to feel familiar?”  
> “What did you miss?”  
> “What did you gain?”  
> “Which language did you speak where?”  
> “Were you treated as an outsider?”  
> “What did you learn to hide or change?”

## Love, marriage, and partnership

Use only with permission.

> “How did people around you understand marriage then?”  
> “What choices did you have?”  
> “How did the relationship begin?”  
> “What did you know about the other person at first?”  
> “What surprised you later?”  
> “What changed after marriage or partnership?”  
> “How were decisions made?”  
> “What was easy to talk about?”  
> “What was hard to talk about?”  
> “How did affection show itself?”  
> “How did conflict show itself?”  
> “What do younger people misunderstand about relationships then?”

## Parenting and caregiving

> “When did you first feel responsible for someone else?”  
> “What kind of parent or caregiver did you hope to be?”  
> “What did you repeat from your elders?”  
> “What did you try to do differently?”  
> “What did children need most?”  
> “What were you proud of?”  
> “What was difficult?”  
> “Who helped you?”  
> “What was invisible about that work?”  
> “What did caregiving change in you?”

## Friendship and community

> “Who were your friends at that time?”  
> “Where did people gather?”  
> “How did people show loyalty?”  
> “Who could you speak freely with?”  
> “Who made you laugh?”  
> “Was there someone outside the family who became like family?”  
> “What did neighbors do for each other?”  
> “What did the community expect from you?”  
> “When did you feel you belonged?”  
> “When did you feel outside?”

## Faith, values, and ethics

> “What religious or spiritual practices were part of daily life?”  
> “Who taught you right and wrong?”  
> “What did people consider sin, shame, duty, or honor?”  
> “Has your belief changed?”  
> “Was there a moment that changed what you believed?”  
> “What did you once believe strongly but no longer believe?”  
> “What value did you hold even when it cost you?”  
> “What do you think people overvalue?”  
> “What do you think people undervalue?”

## Body, health, illness, and aging

Use gently.

> “What did health mean in your family?”  
> “How did people handle illness?”  
> “Who cared for the sick?”  
> “What was not spoken about?”  
> “Was there a period when your body changed your life?”  
> “What did aging feel like at different stages?”  
> “What did you learn from illness or caregiving?”  
> “What should people understand about dependency?”  
> “What has your body allowed you to do?”  
> “What has it taken away?”

## Public events and history

> “Which public events entered your daily life?”  
> “How did news travel?”  
> “What did people argue about?”  
> “Were there events everyone remembers but you experienced differently?”  
> “How did politics affect ordinary life?”  
> “Did law, police, government, war, or policy change your choices?”  
> “What would history books miss about that period?”  
> “What did people know at the time, and what did they only understand later?”

## Culture, language, caste, class, race, gender, and belonging

Adapt to context and ask with care.

> “What identities mattered most in daily life?”  
> “What did people know about someone from their name, accent, clothes, or family?”  
> “What doors opened for you?”  
> “What doors were closed?”  
> “What were you expected to accept?”  
> “When did you first notice inequality?”  
> “What could people say openly?”  
> “What had to be said indirectly?”  
> “Did you ever change yourself to be accepted?”  
> “What do outsiders misunderstand?”

## Humor, play, and mischief

> “Who was the funniest person in your life?”  
> “What joke or phrase still makes you smile?”  
> “What did children do that adults disapproved of?”  
> “What did you get away with?”  
> “What celebrations were most alive?”  
> “What songs did people sing?”  
> “What games did people play?”  
> “What story does the family always laugh about?”  
> “What kind of humor was considered rude or acceptable?”

## Food and domestic life

> “What did an ordinary meal look like?”  
> “Who cooked?”  
> “Who ate first?”  
> “What was saved for guests?”  
> “What food meant celebration?”  
> “What food meant hardship?”  
> “Who taught recipes?”  
> “What tools were used?”  
> “What changed when technology changed?”  
> “Which taste brings back a place or person?”

## Skills, objects, and disappearing knowledge

> “What do you know how to do that younger people may not know?”  
> “Who taught you?”  
> “Can you describe the steps?”  
> “What tools were needed?”  
> “What mistakes did beginners make?”  
> “What was considered skillful?”  
> “Which object in your life has a story?”  
> “What should be preserved before it disappears?”

## Turning points

> “Was there a moment after which life was not the same?”  
> “Did you know at the time that it was a turning point?”  
> “What choices existed?”  
> “Who influenced the decision?”  
> “What did you gain?”  
> “What did you lose?”  
> “What would have happened if you had chosen differently?”  
> “How do you judge that moment now?”

## Failure, regret, and unresolved material

Use only when trust exists.

> “Is there a decision you still think about?”  
> “Is there something you wish people understood more kindly?”  
> “Is there a period you avoid talking about?”  
> “Is there a regret you have made peace with?”  
> “Is there a regret you have not made peace with?”  
> “What remains unresolved?”  
> “What should not be forced into a lesson?”  
> “Would you rather leave this topic here?”

## Pride and invisible achievement

> “What are you proud of that others may not notice?”  
> “What did you survive without being praised for?”  
> “What did you build slowly?”  
> “What responsibility did you carry quietly?”  
> “What did you do because it had to be done?”  
> “What part of your life required courage that nobody saw?”

## Counter-script questions

These are designed to break the standard biography pattern.

> “What part of your life would a normal biography leave out?”  
> “Who mattered greatly but would not appear on a family tree?”  
> “What could someone like you not openly want?”  
> “What did you do every day that nobody thought worth recording?”  
> “What did you know that educated people did not know?”  
> “What did women, workers, children, elders, or outsiders understand that others missed?”  
> “What should we resist simplifying about your life?”  
> “Which version of you never got to exist?”  
> “What would younger people misunderstand about life then?”

## Legacy

> “What do you hope people understand about you?”  
> “What should future family members know?”  
> “What should they not romanticize?”  
> “What warning would you give them?”  
> “What permission would you give them?”  
> “What family habit should continue?”  
> “What family habit should end?”  
> “What question do you wish someone had asked you earlier?”  
> “What would you say directly to someone listening many years from now?”

## Review and close

> “What did I misunderstand?”  
> “What should I ask again?”  
> “What should remain private?”  
> “What should be sealed until later?”  
> “What should be deleted or not used?”  
> “What topic should we return to?”  
> “What feels like a good place to stop today?”
