# Memory Risk and Question Safety

## Why this file exists

An interviewer can accidentally damage the very thing they are trying to preserve.

Memory is influenced by wording, later knowledge, photographs, family stories, emotion, and the social situation of being interviewed. A careless question can introduce a fact that later feels remembered.

The safest method is not silence. It is disciplined questioning.

## What can go wrong

### 1. Leading questions

Bad:

> “How fast was the motorcycle going when it smashed into the wall?”

This suggests intensity and may alter the account.

Safer:

> “What do you remember seeing or hearing?”

### 2. Introduced emotion

Bad:

> “Were you terrified?”

Safer:

> “What do you remember feeling, if anything?”

### 3. Introduced motive

Bad:

> “Why did your uncle betray the family?”

Safer:

> “How did people understand your uncle’s decision then?”

### 4. Introduced causality

Bad:

> “Was caste the reason they refused?”

Safer:

> “What reasons did people give, and what reasons do you think were underneath?”

### 5. Contamination from another account

Bad:

> “Your sister says it happened after the wedding. Do you agree?”

Safer first:

> “When do you place this in your own memory?”

Comparison can happen later.

### 6. Photographic certainty

Bad:

> “This photo shows you were happy there.”

Safer:

> “What do you notice when you see this photo now?”

### 7. False precision

Bad:

> “So this was March 1976?”

Safer:

> “Should we mark this as approximate?”

### 8. Forced lesson

Bad:

> “What did this teach you?”

Safer:

> “How do you understand this now?”

## The safety filter

Before asking a question, check:

1. Did I introduce a new fact?
2. Did I introduce an emotion?
3. Did I introduce a motive?
4. Did I introduce another person’s version?
5. Did I imply the desired answer?
6. Did I ask for exactness the narrator does not have?
7. Did I turn pain into a lesson?
8. Did I ask two or more questions at once?
9. Did I ignore a boundary?
10. Is this question more about my curiosity than their story?

If yes, rewrite.

## Safer question stems

Use:

- “What do you remember about…”
- “How do you remember…”
- “What was it like…”
- “Can you describe…”
- “Can you take me to…”
- “What happened next…”
- “Who was involved…”
- “What choices seemed available…”
- “How did people explain it then…”
- “How do you see it now…”

Avoid:

- “Surely…”
- “You must have…”
- “Were you devastated…”
- “Why did they betray…”
- “Didn’t you…”
- “Isn’t it true that…”
- “Everyone says…”
- “But actually…”

## Unsafe-to-safe examples

| Situation | Unsafe | Safer |
|---|---|---|
| Narrator mentions leaving home | “Were you scared to leave?” | “What do you remember about the day you left?” |
| Narrator says family had little money | “Did poverty make you ashamed?” | “What did money mean in the household then?” |
| Narrator mentions a strict father | “Did he beat you?” | “What did strictness look like in daily life?” |
| Narrator mentions illness | “Was that traumatic?” | “How did that illness affect daily life?” |
| Narrator mentions marriage | “Did you love him?” | “How did the relationship begin and change?” |
| Narrator mentions politics | “Were you against the government?” | “How did politics enter ordinary life?” |
| Narrator mentions a conflict | “Who was right?” | “How did each person understand it then?” |
| Narrator is uncertain | “Are you sure?” | “How certain do you feel about this part?” |

## Handling contradictions

Contradiction is normal in family history.

Possible reasons:

- Different vantage points.
- Different dates.
- Shame or privacy.
- Later family retellings.
- Strategic silence.
- Trauma.
- Ordinary forgetting.
- Real disagreement.
- Different meanings of the same event.

Do not confront during first recall.

Use:

> “Earlier you placed this before the move, and today it sounds like it may have been after. Should we leave both versions for now?”

or:

> “Would you like to revisit the timing, or should we mark it as uncertain?”

## Memory-source labels

When taking notes, mark the source of a claim.

Suggested labels:

- Direct memory.
- Family story.
- Heard from named person.
- Inferred.
- Document-supported.
- Photo-cued.
- Approximate.
- Uncertain.
- Disputed.
- Private.
- Restricted.

Example:

> “The sale of the field happened around 1978 — narrator uncertain; may be family story rather than direct memory.”

## Artifact caution

Artifacts are powerful cues. They can also steer memory.

When using a photo, song, object, or document:

1. Let the narrator respond first.
2. Ask open questions.
3. Record what artifact was present.
4. Do not claim the artifact proves a feeling.
5. Separate document facts from remembered experience.

Example:

> “The letter is dated 1981. What do you remember about receiving it, if anything?”

## What not to optimize

Do not optimize for:

- Tears.
- Confession.
- Drama.
- Total disclosure.
- Neat endings.
- Emotional breakthroughs.
- Agreement between relatives.
- Longest possible interview.
- Most intimate possible question.

Optimize for:

- Narrator control.
- Specific scenes.
- Ordinary-life texture.
- Clear source labeling.
- Context.
- Willingness to continue.
- Honest uncertainty.
