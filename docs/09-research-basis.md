# Research Basis

This file explains the research traditions behind the method and what this project borrows from each.

It is not a comprehensive literature review.

## 1. Oral history

Oral history treats interviews as collaborative records shaped by narrator, interviewer, context, consent, and later use.

What this method borrows:

- Open-ended interviewing.
- Preparation before recording.
- Informed and ongoing consent.
- Respect for narrator agency.
- Preservation of recordings and context.
- Attention to future access and harm.
- Awareness that interviews are co-created.

Useful sources:

- Oral History Association, *Principles and Best Practices*: https://oralhistory.org/best-practices/
- Oral History Association, *Informed Consent*: https://oralhistory.org/informed-consent/
- Smithsonian Archives, *How to Do Oral History*: https://siarchives.si.edu/history/how-do-oral-history

## 2. McAdams’s Life Story Interview

Dan McAdams’s Life Story Interview asks narrators to construct a life as a story, including chapters, key scenes, high points, low points, turning points, challenges, values, and future chapters.

What this method borrows:

- The life-as-chapters framing.
- The idea that life stories are selective, not exhaustive.
- Attention to turning points and key scenes.
- Interest in identity and meaning.
- The distinction between what happened and what it came to mean.

Useful source:

- McAdams, *The Life Story Interview – II*: https://cpb-us-e1.wpmucdn.com/sites.northwestern.edu/dist/4/3901/files/2020/11/The-Life-Story-Interview-II-2007.pdf

## 3. Biographic-Narrative Interpretive Method

BNIM uses a broad opening prompt and gives the narrator space to produce their own initial narrative before follow-up questioning.

What this method borrows:

- Start with a broad invitation.
- Let the narrator structure the first account.
- Follow up using the narrator’s own words and sequence.
- Avoid imposing the interviewer’s categories too early.

Useful sources:

- Corbally and O’Neill, *An introduction to the biographical narrative interpretive method*: https://doras.dcu.ie/20033/2/BNIM_paper_for_DORAS.pdf
- Wengraf, short guide to BNIM: https://eprints.ncrm.ac.uk/id/eprint/30/1/Biographic-NarrativeInterpretiveMethodShortGuide.doc

## 4. Life History Calendar and life grid methods

Life History Calendar methods help people reconstruct timelines by aligning life domains such as residence, education, work, family, health, and public events.

What this method borrows:

- Use time cues and domain cues.
- Allow multiple starting points.
- Treat chronology as a scaffold, not the whole story.
- Track uncertainty explicitly.
- Link events across life domains.

Useful sources:

- Nelson, *Adapting the Life History Calendar Method*: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/1525822X10379793
- Chevallereau et al., *Quality principles of retrospective data collected through a life history calendar*: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9612623/
- Rowland et al., *Using the Life Grid Interview Technique in Science Education Research*: https://arxiv.org/abs/1903.02386

## 5. Autobiographical memory

Autobiographical memory is reconstructive. It is shaped by self-image, later knowledge, repeated retelling, cultural scripts, family narratives, and the interview context.

What this method borrows:

- Avoid leading questions.
- Preserve uncertainty.
- Separate direct memory from family story.
- Avoid contaminating one narrator’s account with another narrator’s account.
- Treat detail as meaningful, not automatically true.
- Preserve the original wording.

Useful sources:

- Conway and Pleydell-Pearce, *The construction of autobiographical memories in the self-memory system*: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/10789197/
- Loftus and Palmer, *Reconstruction of automobile destruction*: https://www.demenzemedicinagenerale.net/images/mens-sana/AutomobileDestruction.pdf
- Chan et al., *Conversational AI Powered by Large Language Models Amplifies False Memories in Witness Interviews*: https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.04681

## 6. Cultural life scripts

People remember and narrate lives partly through culturally expected milestones. These scripts can help organize memory but also hide what does not fit the expected biography.

What this method borrows:

- Do not assume one universal life path.
- Ask counter-script questions.
- Look for culturally specific roles, duties, kinship, shame, honor, migration, faith, caste, class, gender, and language.
- Preserve non-standard stories without forcing them into a familiar arc.

Useful source:

- Berntsen and Rubin, *Cultural life scripts structure recall from autobiographical memory*: https://link.springer.com/article/10.3758/BF03195836

## 7. Reminiscence and functions of memory

Remembering serves many functions: identity, bonding, teaching, problem-solving, emotional regulation, legacy, and social belonging. Not all reminiscence is beneficial. Some remembering can become bitter, repetitive, or distressing.

What this method borrows:

- Ask why a story matters, not only what happened.
- Capture social and teaching functions of memory.
- Avoid assuming every painful memory should be explored.
- Include humor, ordinary life, skill, and transmission.

Useful sources:

- Bluck et al., *A TALE of three functions: The self-reported uses of autobiographical memory*: https://dukespace.lib.duke.edu/items/c35bbb7e-5699-4279-9fc5-cf3d1d9dc8e2
- Webster, *Construction and validation of the Reminiscence Functions Scale*: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/8366271/
- Westerhof et al., *Reminiscence and mental health*: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/ageing-and-society/article/reminiscence-and-mental-health-a-review-of-recent-progress-in-theory-research-and-interventions/A92B78BECF29F386309138DCBED28531

## 8. Interpersonal closeness and progressive disclosure

The “36 questions” work associated with Aron and colleagues showed that structured reciprocal self-disclosure can increase interpersonal closeness in an experimental setting.

What this method borrows:

- Gradual deepening.
- Pacing.
- Movement from easier to more personal prompts.
- The importance of responsiveness.

What this method does not borrow:

- A fixed universal sequence.
- The goal of producing closeness in one session.
- The assumption that vulnerability is always good.
- The popular myth that 36 questions “make people fall in love.”

Useful source:

- Aron et al., *The Experimental Generation of Interpersonal Closeness*: https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/0146167297234003

## 9. Trauma-informed practice

Trauma-informed practice emphasizes safety, trust, choice, collaboration, empowerment, and attention to cultural, historical, and gender issues.

What this method borrows:

- Narrator control.
- Permission before sensitive topics.
- No forced disclosure.
- No promise of healing.
- Stop and pause options.
- Gentle closure after difficult material.
- Awareness of power differences.

Useful sources:

- SAMHSA trauma-informed approaches: https://www.samhsa.gov/mental-health/trauma-violence/trauma-informed-approaches-programs
- SAMHSA concept paper PDF: https://www.health.ny.gov/health_care/medicaid/program/medicaid_health_homes/docs/samhsa_trauma_concept_paper.pdf

## 10. Life-course sociology

Life-course thinking emphasizes historical time, timing of events, agency within constraint, linked lives, and cumulative advantage or disadvantage.

What this method borrows:

- Ask how historical time shaped the narrator.
- Ask what choices were available, not only what choices were made.
- Treat lives as connected to other lives.
- Track timing: the same event matters differently at age 15, 35, or 70.
- Look for institutions, public events, policy, migration, war, economy, caste, class, and gender.

Useful source:

- Glen Elder’s life-course research overview: https://elder.web.unc.edu/research-projects/

## 11. Why this is not a psychometric questionnaire

This method is not trying to produce a numerical trait score.

It is an applied qualitative interview method.

Evaluation should focus on:

- Narrator control.
- Specific episodes captured.
- Breadth across life domains.
- Ordinary-life texture.
- Question safety.
- Preservation of uncertainty.
- Contextual richness.
- Narrator approval of representation.
- Willingness to continue.

## 12. Working research claim

The core claim to test is:

> A trained human interviewer using a semi-structured life-narrative method can elicit richer, safer, more contextual life stories than a fixed prompt list alone, while preserving narrator agency better than an interrogation-style questionnaire.

This is a claim to study, not a fact to assume.
