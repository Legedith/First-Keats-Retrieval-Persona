# Facilitator Training

This method depends on the interviewer.

A good question bank used by a careless interviewer can still cause harm. A thoughtful interviewer with a small set of principles can do good work.

## Training goals

A trained interviewer should be able to:

1. Explain the purpose of the interview.
2. Obtain and revisit consent.
3. Ask one open question at a time.
4. Follow the narrator’s language.
5. Move from summary to scene.
6. Ask about context without imposing interpretation.
7. Avoid leading questions.
8. Handle silence.
9. Respond safely to distress.
10. Track coverage and uncertainty.
11. End sessions well.
12. Accept refusal.

## Training exercise 1: Rewrite leading questions

Rewrite each question.

1. “Were you ashamed when you lost the job?”
2. “Why did your father abandon the family?”
3. “Did poverty make you angry?”
4. “Your sister says you were always stubborn. Is that true?”
5. “Was your marriage unhappy?”
6. “Did caste ruin your chances?”
7. “Were you traumatized by that accident?”

Good rewrites should remove assumed facts, motives, and emotions.

Example:

Unsafe:

> “Were you ashamed when you lost the job?”

Safer:

> “What do you remember about the period after the job ended?”

## Training exercise 2: Practice silence

In pairs:

1. Ask a broad question.
2. After the answer, wait five seconds before asking the next question.
3. Notice whether the narrator continues.
4. Discuss how the silence felt.

Many interviewers interrupt because silence makes them uncomfortable, not because the narrator is finished.

## Training exercise 3: Follow the narrator’s word

Narrator says:

> “That was the year the house became cold.”

The interviewer must generate five safe follow-ups using the word “cold” without interpreting it.

Examples:

> “What do you mean by cold?”  
> “Can you remember a day when the house felt cold in that way?”  
> “Who else would have noticed that coldness?”  
> “Did the coldness change later?”  
> “How do you understand that word now?”

## Training exercise 4: Summary to scene

Convert broad statements into scene prompts.

Broad:

> “My mother was strong.”

Scene prompt:

> “Can you remember one moment that shows her strength?”

Broad:

> “We had nothing.”

Scene prompt:

> “Can you take me to an ordinary day when that was visible?”

Broad:

> “The city changed me.”

Scene prompt:

> “Can you remember a day when you first felt changed by the city?”

## Training exercise 5: Stop well

Practice responding to:

> “I do not want to talk about that.”

Good response:

> “Of course. We can leave that out. Would you like to move to another topic or pause?”

Bad response:

> “Only if you are comfortable, but it would really help future generations understand.”

The second response pressures the narrator.

## Training exercise 6: Coverage review

After a mock interview, mark:

- Which life period was covered?
- Was there a specific scene?
- Was there an ordinary-day account?
- Were relationships explored?
- Was social context explored?
- Was uncertainty preserved?
- Were there any leading questions?
- Did the narrator remain in control?

## Advanced interviewer habits

Skilled interviewers notice:

- What the narrator repeats.
- What the narrator avoids.
- Which names appear without explanation.
- Which years are dense and which are empty.
- Where ordinary life is missing.
- When the interviewer’s own relationship is shaping the answer.
- When a question would satisfy curiosity but not serve the narrator.
- When a lighter question is better than a deeper one.

## Supervision and review

If this becomes a serious project, interviewers should review sample transcripts with others.

Review for:

- Leading questions.
- Missed follow-ups.
- Over-talking.
- Poor pacing.
- Failure to ask about ordinary life.
- Failure to ask about social context.
- Boundary violations.
- Assumed redemption or lesson.
- Mishandled uncertainty.

Do not only review whether the interview “felt emotional.” That is not enough.
