# Good vs Bad Questions

This file gives practical examples of safer question design.

## Rule

A bad question often contains the interviewer’s theory.

A good question invites the narrator’s account.

## Example 1: Leaving home

Bad:

> “Were you scared when you left home?”

Problem:

- Assumes fear.

Better:

> “What do you remember about the day you left home?”

Possible follow-up:

> “What did you think was going to happen?”

## Example 2: Strict parent

Bad:

> “Did your father beat you?”

Problem:

- Introduces violence.
- May be too abrupt.
- May pressure disclosure.

Better:

> “You described him as strict. What did strictness look like in daily life?”

Possible follow-up:

> “Were there things children could not say or do?”

## Example 3: Poverty

Bad:

> “Did being poor make you ashamed?”

Problem:

- Assumes poverty.
- Assumes shame.

Better:

> “What did money mean in your household then?”

Possible follow-up:

> “What was saved, borrowed, repaired, or reused?”

## Example 4: Marriage

Bad:

> “Did you love your husband?”

Problem:

- Too blunt.
- May impose a modern romantic frame.
- May be unsafe depending on relationship and culture.

Better:

> “How did your relationship begin?”

Possible follow-up:

> “How did it change over time?”

## Example 5: Historical event

Bad:

> “Did Partition traumatize everyone?”

Problem:

- Overgeneralizes.
- Assumes trauma.
- Uses clinical language.

Better:

> “How did that period enter daily life in your family?”

Possible follow-up:

> “What did people talk about openly, and what was left unsaid?”

## Example 6: Family conflict

Bad:

> “Who was right?”

Problem:

- Turns story into judgment.

Better:

> “How did each person understand the conflict then?”

Possible follow-up:

> “Do you understand it differently now?”

## Example 7: Other people’s versions

Bad:

> “Your sister said this happened because of money. Is that true?”

Problem:

- Contaminates independent recall.
- Puts narrator on defense.

Better:

> “What do you remember about why it happened?”

Later, after independent accounts:

> “There are different memories of this event. Would you like to compare them, or should we keep your account separate?”

## Example 8: Regret

Bad:

> “Do you regret not studying more?”

Problem:

- Assumes regret.

Better:

> “How do you think about your education now?”

Possible follow-up:

> “Were there paths you wished were available?”

## Example 9: Illness

Bad:

> “Was that the worst time of your life?”

Problem:

- Forces ranking and drama.

Better:

> “What changed in daily life during that illness?”

Possible follow-up:

> “Who helped most?”

## Example 10: Closing

Bad:

> “Is there anything else?”

Problem:

- Too vague.
- Often causes the narrator to end without reflection.

Better:

> “What did I fail to ask that matters?”  
> “Is there anything from today that should remain private?”  
> “What would feel like a good place to stop?”
